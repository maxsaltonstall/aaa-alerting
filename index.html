Presentation Summary

Modern Observability for Distributed Systems

This session provided a deep dive into the evolution from traditional monitoring to modern observability. We explored why today's complex, distributed architectures demand a more proactive and insightful approach to system health. For early-career engineers, mastering these concepts is crucial for building resilient, performant, and debuggable applications. The talk focused on the three pillars of observability—logs, metrics, and traces—and emphasized how their integration provides the context needed to truly understand system behavior, not just observe its symptoms.

Key Takeaways for Engineers

Click on each point to expand and see why it matters.

Monitoring tells you *when* something is wrong (e.g., CPU is at 90%). Observability lets you ask *why* it's wrong by exploring the system's state. It's the difference between having a fixed dashboard of known failure states and having the tools to investigate unknown, novel problems—which are common in microservices.

Your Action: Shift your mindset from "Is the system up?" to "Can I understand what the system is doing right now?"

Logs, metrics, and traces are powerful, but their true value is unlocked when they are linked together. A spike in a metric (like error rate) should lead you directly to the traces representing those failed requests, which in turn should link to the specific logs containing the error details.

Your Action: When instrumenting code, ensure you're adding metadata (like `trace_id` and `span_id`) to your logs so your tools can automatically correlate these signals.

Plain text logs like `"User 123 failed to update profile"` are hard to query and analyze at scale. Structured logs (e.g., JSON format) like `{"event": "profile_update_failed", "user_id": 123, "reason": "db_timeout"}` turn your logs into a rich, queryable dataset. You can easily filter, aggregate, and create metrics from them.

Your Action: Use a logging library that supports structured output by default. Always log key-value pairs instead of embedding variables in strings.

An isolated error log is just noise. An error log with context—like the user ID, request trace, application version, and host environment—is an actionable insight. Distributed tracing is the ultimate tool for providing this context, showing the full journey of a request as it travels through multiple services.

Your Action: Propagate context (like trace headers) across all service calls. Enrich your logs and metrics with meaningful tags and attributes that will help you slice and dice the data later.
